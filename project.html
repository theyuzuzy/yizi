Journey To The Moon (2022)

Role: Project Manager & Graphic Designer

Journey to the Moon is an interactive installation that shares Chinatown’s history, under the theme of the Mid-Autumn Festival. With both an adult and children’s version, this mobile application (app) uses augmented reality (AR) to feature a 3 dimensional (3D) model of Chinatown.

Trailer

    To honour the cultural significance of Chinatown in Melbourne, we proposed an app that would showcase a 3D model of Chinatown, upon scanning a physical map via the camera.
  • Information about the historical sites can be accessed through AR and within the app.
  • In the adult version, an itinerary is generated based on the historical sites they are interested in.
  • In the children’s version, there is a moon rabbit mascot and animation, inspired by the Mid-Autumn Festival tale.

Key Graphics & Animation

Challenges

Everyone had a different understanding of the proposed approach for the project:

  • I wrote the proposed approach into our documentations and Trello board, to make it accessible.
  • Frequent team meetings to discuss the project’s proposed approach were also held.
  • If our understandings misligned, I would listen and see which aspects were different, and remind them of the approach we had agreed on.
  • When we had to reiterate the proposed approach, to improve the design, I encouraged everyone’s input. I included the ideas in the proposed approach, while ensuring the proposal would not be overwhelming.

Content tailored to Adult and Children’s Versions:

  • Catering to personas of different ages and backgrounds with both an adult and children’s version, we needed to customise the information to suit the different demographics. Thus, information was simplified into dot points for the children’s version.

Animation:

  • I studied the movements of rabbits and learnt about different types of frames, including keyframes, in-between frames and extreme frames, to create the animation successfully.

As the Project Manager:

  • I was responsible for managing the production of the required deliverables
  • I planned and monitored the project, delegating roles and tasks to every team member
  • I encouraged input from everyone, listened attentively to every idea and ensured our workloads were fair and balanced

As the Graphic Designer:

  • I researched, sketched various designs and evaluated on them, based on the style guide, as well as the feedback of my teammates
  • These designs included the logo, moon rabbit mascot, map of Chinatown with locked and unlocked historical sites and loading bar
  • I also made a storyboard and animated the moon rabbit’s journey to the moon.

Paw & Footprints (2023)

Role: UX/UI Designer & Video Editor

Paw & Footprints is an initiative that encourages small actions that result in collective impact on tackling climate change. Visually appealing and collectable endangered species unlock, when users complete 4 daily environmentally-conscious tasks.

Trailer

As a form of activism, Paw & Footprints is a mobile application that aims to:
  • Educate the user on the species that are at risk due to climate change
  • Mobilise the user to take small individual actions that creates collective impact
  • Engages the user with appealing and colourful illustrations of endangered and extinct species
  • Motivate users with a record of all the actions they have taken to support the environment

  • When four daily tasks are completed, a specimen is unlocked at random and there is a ‘Learn More’ button that leads to more information about the unlocked specimen.
  • The tasks are randomised each day and are environmentally conscious. They are simple and set at home, so users can develop green habits.
  • Graphics are colourful and textured, so the users are not intimidated when using the app. The rough style also aligns with the purpose of the app, where despite the actions seeming small, a collective impact is achieved.
  • A record of all the actions users have taken each day, is presented in a creative and organised manner. They can easily see how many tasks have been completed on each day and week, at a glance.

Challenges

Design of icons:

  • Instead of standard icons, I wanted to customise the icons, incorporating paws and footprints, to fit the context of the app.
  • This led to many variations and experimentations, before I achieved designs that are unique, yet easy to see and understand at a glance.

Design of the History Page:

  • The History Page includes a record of the user’s completed tasks in the past. This comprises a lot of information, such as the exact day, date, daily overview, weekly overview and details about the tasks.
  • I explored different layouts that would showcase all the information in one interface, as I wanted to avoid unnecessary clicks for the user.
  • The visual design of the weekly overview shows what species have been unlocked and how many tasks were completed each day at a glance.

Sound design:

  • Initially, there were no sound effects, diminishing the user experience. Thus, I added sounds of species in the information content cards, so users can understand more about the species, by listening to them as well.
  • I also added a success sound effect whenever they unlock species, to create an audiovisual cue.

As the UX/UI Designer:

  • I identified my personas, created their profiles and scenarios
  • I created an empathy map, journey map and user flow to prepare for my prototype process
  • I established the style guide and designed the visual elements, including the buttons, icons, animations and graphics on Procreate
  • I researched extensively into climate change, impacts on endangered species, and information about certain species that are featured in my mockups and prototype
  • I created low and high-fidelity wireframes, mockups and prototypes

As the Video Editor:

  • I watched other app trailers to gain a better understanding of the video structure and flow
  • I created a storyboard for the app trailer
  • I created most visual elements of the video, such as screen recordings of the prototype in action, as well as text against coloured backgrounds
  • I sourced other visual elements, including screenshots of news articles about climate change and video footage of pollution on news sites and Pixabay
  • I sourced audio elements from Pixabay that were appropriate for the trailer, which work to create the transition between a sombre to hopeful feeling
  • I cut certain parts of the visual elements to match the beats of the audio at the start to spark viewer’s interest
  • I adjusted the volumes of the audio elements to create and maintain the emotional appeal

Tech-Neck-Logy Timer (2023)

Role: UX Researcher, UX/UI Designer & Graphic Designer

Tech-Neck-Logy Timer is a solution to a common problem in the 21st century - ‘Tech Neck’. As a time-based mobile application, users are reminded to reset and maintain good posture with customisable notifications and a guide.

Challenges

Types of notifications:

  • In my initial design of the app, there was only one type of notification, which was setting the days and one specific time that would remind users to practise posture. I gained feedback about it being limited in its ability to effectively remind users on correcting posture.
  • Thus, I designed two types of notifications, where one repeats at set intervals and the other occurring at specific times. This challenged how I showcased them visually, so I experimented with various wordings and UI.
  • The two notifications are confusing and thus, require lines, to separate them visually.

Colour palette:

  • I tried to create a playful app, however the minimalistic colour scheme of greens and a contrasting hue of reddish-brown failed to present this feeling. Further, the reddish-brown was used on the page where the user gives up on practising posture. I realised it would discourage the user with this colour. I decided to change it and ensure there are no negative connotations.

Design of Home Page:

  • There is a ‘tap for more’ button on the middle left of the page. This is the result of my various attempts of showcasing what durations are suited for what types of people, such as 1 minute for busy people and 5 minutes for those who have more time.
  • However, this button seems out of place and creates an imbalance in the visual hierarchy. As a result, I decided to move the short descriptions of the different durations into the interface when the timer starts.

As the UX Researcher:

  • I researched about the challenge of poor posture, its causes and solutions
  • I shared my personal experience and interviewed two people about their experiences, to create an experience map
  • I tested the prototype on 15 people, through 5 second and first click tests, and these results were used to improve the prototype

As the UX/UI Designer:

  • I created wireflows, wireframes and conducted two rounds of prototype testing
  • I made iterations to the wireflows and wireframes based on the feedback and results
  • I made sure to include information needs and patterns
  • I created a design system, focusing on visual hierarchy, moodboard and aesthetic styles

HEMWAYS (2023)

Role: UI Designer

HEMWAYS is an immersive experience that transports users into the laneways of Melbourne and Ho Chi Minh City through their screens. With both a mobile and desktop version, users can see, listen, interact and learn about the two cultures in Bui Vien Walking Street and Degraves Street.

Walkthrough

Challenges

Type of media format for the visuals:

  • Initially, we proposed still-images presented through parallax scrolling. However, we gained feedback that this format limits the interactivity and creates a sense of division between the two cities.
  • As we wanted to focus on the link between the two cities and their laneway cultures, where they are similar despite their differences, we came up with either 360 degree video or panoramic imagery.
  • We agreed on the latter, as it would prevent cognitive overload and overwhelm from too much movement in the footage.

Setting the two laneways apart visually:

  • While we wanted to show similarities between the cities, users should also be able to distinguish between them. Therefore, we implemented light and dark modes.
  • The content cards and the navigation bar in Bui Vien Walking Street will be presented in a dark mode, whereas Degraves Street is in light mode.
  • Bui Vien Walking Street in dark mode also highlights its vibrant and bustling nightlife, which contrasts with Degraves Street in light mode, which showcases its rush hours and coffee runs in the morning.

Aesthetic of the website’s imagery:

  • Initially, we wanted to present the graphics in a flat style, using Adobe Illustrator. However, the aesthetic did not match the detailed elements of the laneways.
  • As a result, we changed to a semi-realistic style, using Procreate, that references the graffiti and street art present in both cities.
  • This style was achieved in Procreate, as the iPad was able to capture the natural strokes, reminiscent of street art. The style is able to stand out to attract the attention of the users, and is supported by outlines that also suggest clickability.

As the UI Designer and Graphic Designer:

  • I researched about the laneways and the cultures in Ho Chi Minh City and Melbourne to make initial sketches
  • I designed, sought feedback from my teammates, and made the appropriate changes based on the feedback
  • These designs were the clickable street signs on the Home Page and interactable objects throughout the panoramic sceneries
  • I also designed most of the slides for the many presentations used to update on the progress of our project

As a member of the group:

  • I organised group meetings, initiated and maintained communication with all my team members
  • I ensured everyone had the same understanding of the project, that we had fair and balanced workloads, and presented alongside each other
  • I strove to research in depth about the two cultures for the proposal and the information used for the content cards, analyse case studies thoroughly, create the early wireframes, help establish the style guide, plan the site layout and make design considerations to promote accessibility
